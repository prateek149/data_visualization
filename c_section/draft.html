C-Section Rates: Too Low or Too High

- C-sections are needed to deal with complicated delivery cases, and could help save lives. C-Section delivery needs to be performed by a qualified surgeon/gynaecologist and so c-section rate is, in some ways, a proxy of access to maternal care.

The following map displays the rates of C-section deliveries across all the districts in India. Aside from Gujarat, and Jammu and Kahsmir, for which no data is available in any of the major health surveys in India, there are substantial variations in the number of ceasarean deliveries across the country. Telengana (on the extreme high end) and parts of Rajasthan, M.P, Chattisgarh stand out for low levels of ceasarean deliveries.

What is driving the high c-section rate?

- The common perception is that the private sector is driving up the c-section rate. Reasons for this
---- Information asymmetry between the doctor and the patient leading to over provision of elective surgeries
---- Private sector responding to the high demand for c-section

- To some extent, the data corroborates this. In 85% of the districts, c-section rates among the deliveries conducted in private institutions is more than 20%. Same is true for only 12% of the districts in case of deliveries conducted at public institutions.

- C-section rates in private institutions are high across the board. And so the variation that we see the aggregate c-section rates across in India depend on two factors
---- % of deliveries happening at private institutions
---- % of c-section deliveries in public institutions

- And we see both of these factors prevalent in southern states

- In more developed states, woman (or rather households) are choosing private institutions for delivery. Even though cost of delivery is much higher in private institutions

- NSS Round 71 data, gives us a sense why people are choosing private over public institutions. Household members who were ill over the past 15 days, and sought treatment at a private facility, were asked why they didn’t go to a govt. facility
---- 43% of them felt that the quality of govt facility was not good
---- 27% cited long wait times
---- 12% said the facility was too far
---- 10% said that no government facility was available.

- As such, combination of access and perceived quality of care could be driving people to private institutions

What can we conclude?

- The government has an overarching focus on building more facilities but all that investment would not improve outcomes if the quality, and the perception of quality, does not improve.

- It is estimated that as high as 70% of people go to a private health service provider. And, they often do a good job of providing services that public infrastructure is failing to provide. So regulating the private sector should be high on their agenda.
